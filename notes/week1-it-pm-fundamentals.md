- Project management is the process of planning, organizing, and managing the execution of a project to achieve specific goals within a set timeline, budget, and scope. Project management involves coordinating resources, managing risks, and ensuring that all tasks and activities are completed efficiently and effectively.
- Projects require a structured approach to be successful.
- Without a clear plan, defined goals, and assigned responsibilities, a project can quickly become disorganized, leading to miscommunication, incomplete tasks, and delays.
- “A goal without a plan is just a wish.”  Antoine de Saint-Exupéry, The Little Prince (Le Petit Prince)
- Project management defines clear objectives and a roadmap to achieve them. By setting priorities and outlining specific tasks, project management eliminates confusion and keeps the team aligned with the project’s goals.
- Every project has limited resources: time, money, and people. Project management ensures these resources are allocated efficiently, avoiding waste and maximizing impact.
- Every project faces unexpected challenges. Project management helps identify potential risks early, so that the team can create backup plans and minimize disruptions to avoid delays.
- Effective project management improves collaboration by ensuring that team members understand their roles and can access the information they need. Regular updates and feedback loops keep the team connected and in agreement.
- With clear goals and milestones, project management ensures that a project’s success is measurable. It provides stakeholders with confidence that the project will deliver value.

- A project manager (PM) is a professional who is responsible for planning, executing, and overseeing a project to ensure the team successfully completes it. A PM coordinates the team, manages resources, and monitors progress to achieve specific goals within a set time frame and budget.

- PMs need strong leadership skills to inspire and guide their team, align efforts toward achieving project goals, and maintain motivation, especially during complex or high-pressure situations.
- PMs apply analytical thinking to evaluate data, assess risks, and develop effective strategies. This helps them make informed decisions that lead to successful project outcomes.
- Effective communication allows PMs to keep stakeholders and team members aligned, clarify roles and responsibilities, and prevent misunderstandings that might hinder progress.
- PMs rely on adaptability to navigate unexpected challenges or changes. This enables them to adjust plans and priorities, as needed, to ensure the project progresses as planned.

- PMs apply problem-solving skills to identify and resolve challenges efficiently, ensuring that progress is not disrupted, and the project team achieves its objectives.
- Stakeholders are individuals, groups, or organizations who have an interest in or are affected by the outcome of a project. Stakeholders can influence the project or be influenced by its results.
- For any project, the number of stakeholders can vary, ranging from a small internal team to a large external group of partners and beneficiaries, each with their own objectives for the project.

- Internal stakeholders are individuals or groups directly involved in the planning, execution, and management of a project. They are typically part of the organization or team working on the project and have an active role in ensuring its success.
- External stakeholders are individuals or groups outside the immediate project team or organization. They have an interest in the project’s outcome but are not directly involved in its daily activities or decision making.
- Stakeholder interests refer to the specific needs and expectations that different individuals or groups have with a project. These interests can vary significantly depending on the stakeholder’s role, objectives, and the way the project’s outcome affects their goals.
- For PMs, understanding stakeholder interests enables them to manage stakeholders effectively while ensuring the successful completion of a project.
- Organizational growth

Internal stakeholders are invested in a project’s success because it reflects their contributions and the organization’s overall performance.

For team members, a successful project can lead to recognition and job security. For senior management, project success means ensuring the organization remains competitive and efficient and can achieve strategic objectives.
- Efficient use of resources

Internal stakeholders want to ensure that the necessary tools, staff, and budget are allocated appropriately to meet project deadlines and goals. They want to avoid situations where resources are overburdened.

Efficient allocation maximizes the chances of a successful project outcome and helps team members maintain productivity.
- Career development

For team members, the opportunity to be part of a successful project can contribute to their career development. These stakeholders might view a project as a chance to gain new skills, showcase their abilities, and advance their professional growth.

A well-executed project might lead to promotions or new opportunities, which can increase the team’s motivation to perform better.
- Quality and performance

External stakeholders, particularly customers, are focused on the quality, functionality, and overall performance of the product or service the project is delivering. They want to ensure that the project meets their needs and expectations, whether that’s usability, durability, or overall satisfaction.

For them, a successful project adds value to their business operations.
- Financial gains

Investors and financial stakeholders are interested in the project’s ability to generate profit, reduce costs, or identify new revenue opportunities.

A project’s financial stability is crucial to them because it affects their investment strategy, and they want the project to contribute to their long-term financial growth.
- Legal and safety requirements

External stakeholders, such as customers, regulatory bodies, and industry standards organizations, are interested in ensuring the project complies with laws, regulations, and standards. This is important for projects in different industries, such as healthcare, construction, or technology, where safety and legal compliance are critical.

These stakeholders want to minimize legal risks and protect public safety, so their interest lies in ensuring the project complies with the required standards.
- Fair and consistent terms
Suppliers and vendors are interested in ensuring that the project adheres to the specified terms, such as pricing, delivery schedules, and payment timelines.

A successful project allows them to strengthen long-term partnerships, which can help them generate revenue and improve their operational efficiency.
- Business relationships
External stakeholders, such as clients, business partners, and collaborators, are invested in building and maintaining strong, long-term relationships. They seek transparency, open communication, and mutual respect throughout the project.

A well-managed project helps build trust, strengthen business relationships, and set the foundation for future opportunities, ensuring that all parties can continue to collaborate effectively beyond the current project.

- A project management lifecycle is a series of phases that guide a project from initiation to completion. These phases help PMs plan, execute, monitor, and finalize work by ensuring that teams stay aligned with the project goals, managing resources effectively, and mitigating risks.
- Phase 1: Initiate and plan a project
PMs begin a project by defining its purpose, scope, and objectives, and obtaining client approval. They create a project charter and identify key stakeholders. They also develop a project roadmap that outlines a project plan with timelines and a resource plan specifying the team members. PMs also define the project’s goals, establish success criteria, and assess risks to create a risk management plan.
- Phase 2: Execute the project
PMs conduct a project kickoff meeting to communicate the project’s details, such as scope, timelines, and team members’ roles and responsibilities. Teams then begin working on their assigned tasks, coordinating to ensure smooth execution. The second phase is where most of the project work happens, and PMs facilitate this work by managing teams, ensuring that the teams have resources to produce high-quality deliverables.
- Phase 3: Monitor and control the project
PMs manage the third phase along with the second phase. They focus on aligning the teams with the project’s goals and addressing any challenges that might impact the timelines or costs. They conduct regular meetings with teams to track progress and measure performance. If required, PMs adjust the project plan and help teams reprioritize their tasks. PMs also communicate progress to stakeholders. By the end of this phase, teams complete the required deliverables for client review.
- Phase 4: Close the project
PMs and teams finalize the project by delivering the completed work to the client. They ensure proper documentation, archive project files, and conduct a review session to document key learnings. PMs acknowledge the team’s efforts, celebrate the project’s completion, and release team members for future assignments.
- A project management framework is a standardized set of guidelines, processes, and tools that helps PMs and teams work consistently and efficiently throughout all phases of a project while staying aligned with the project goals.

- Waterfall is a traditional and sequential project management framework where the project teams complete tasks in several phases that follow a linear order. The teams fully complete each phase before moving to the next phase, making it a structured and predictable approach.
- <img width="1338" height="587" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/587fc251-6822-4993-baa9-f918dcb69dd8" />
- Phase 1: Analyze requirements
PMs work closely with clients and stakeholders to understand the project’s needs, expectations, and constraints. They collect and document all project requirements in a project scope document.
- Phase 2: Design
After finalizing the requirements, the project team creates a detailed design. Based on the project, this might include interface design, technical specifications, or physical layouts.
- Phase 3: Develop
Once the client and stakeholders approve the design, the project team uses the design to create a working product according to the specifications.
- Phase 4: Test
Next, the quality team tests the product to ensure it meets all specified requirements, checking its functionality and performance through various testing methods.
- Phase 5: Deploy
When the project team ensures that the product is free of errors, they launch the product and deliver it to the client and stakeholders.
- The Waterfall framework helps teams complete each phase thoroughly before moving forward, creating a step-by-step path to project completion. For PMs, this framework is a reliable approach that is ideal for projects with fixed requirements and minimal expected changes.
- 
- Agile is a flexible framework that values adaptability, continuous improvement, and a high level of collaboration. It enables project teams to break down work into small, manageable iterations, each resulting in a functional version of the product. This iterative framework allows teams to adapt quickly and respond to changes while prioritizing customer feedback to ensure the product evolves according to user needs.
- Agile is most effective for projects where teams need to adapt quickly, incorporate ongoing feedback, and deliver work in small, manageable chunks, such as:

Software development, where project requirements often evolve

Creative projects that involve iterative design and feedback

Research and development, where experimentation and adaptability are important

- Conceptualize and initiate
The PMs and project teams work with stakeholders to define the overall goals and scope of the project. They focus on understanding the project’s vision, key objectives, and potential challenges. Instead of planning for the long term, they outline high-level deliverables and set expectations.
- Plan for iterative development
In Agile, project teams replace long-term detailed planning with incremental or progressive delivery of value. This means that they divide a project into manageable sections, where they deliver specific features in a short cycle. These cycles allow for flexibility and frequent adjustments based on ongoing feedback.
- Develop and execute
The project team works in short cycles to build and test the product, ensuring they develop, review, and refine each feature before moving to the next. The team collaborates frequently to ensure the product aligns with the project’s vision and goals. Continuous communication allows them to quickly adjust the product based on new insights and challenges.
- Review and gather feedback
At the end of each development cycle, the team presents the completed features to stakeholders or users to gather their feedback. This feedback is essential for identifying improvement areas and deciding the next set of features or changes to prioritize.
- Release and improve continuously
In Agile, project teams continuously improve and refine a product throughout the project lifecycle. As they complete features, they release them to users and gather feedback for future improvements. The team continues to iterate on the product even after its initial release, ensuring it evolves based on real user needs.
- Agile is not a one-time process: it’s an ongoing journey of adapting and responding to change. The project team repeats the cycle of development, review, and improvement as many times as needed to refine the product based on user feedback, evolving requirements, and new insights. This helps the team deliver a product that is closely aligned with user needs and business goals.
- Hybrid is a flexible framework that blends elements of both Waterfall and Agile. This allows PMs to tailor their strategy to best suit the specific needs, using the strengths of each framework.

- the various factors that help PMs choose the best framework to align with their project’s unique characteristics and requirements.


Project complexity and size: PMs must assess whether the project is large and complex, which might require a structured framework, such as Waterfall, or smaller and more flexible, such as Agile.


Stakeholder involvement: PMs need to consider the level of stakeholder engagement required. Agile might be suitable for projects that need frequent stakeholder interaction. Alternatively, Waterfall might be better for projects with less frequent or more formal stakeholder engagement, where changes are less likely during the project lifecycle.


Project scope and deliverables: PMs benefit from evaluating whether the project scope is well-defined or likely to change. Waterfall is ideal for fixed scopes, while Agile and Hybrid approaches work well for projects with evolving deliverables.


Time and resource constraints: PMs should consider the project’s time and budget limitations. Waterfall might be appropriate for projects with tight schedules, while Agile can offer flexibility when resources or deadlines shift.

By considering these factors, PMs can make a more informed decision, ensuring that the chosen framework not only fits the project’s needs but also supports the team in delivering successful outcomes.

- The project management triangle: Scope, time, and cost
- The project management triangle illustrates the relationship among three key factors that influence a project’s outcome: scope, time, and cost. These three factors are interrelated and determine the overall quality of a project.
- Cost
Cost refers to the budget allocated for all project resources, such as labor, materials, and equipment.
- Scope
Scope refers to the work required to complete a project.
- Time
Time represents the project schedule that includes milestones and deadlines.
- These three factors are interconnected because changes in one factor typically require changes in one or both of the other factors. This is why project managers must carefully balance these factors to achieve the best possible quality so that the project can:

•
Remain within budget.

•
Be completed by the deadline.

•
Adhere to the specifications and requirements.

- In project management, effort is the total amount of work needed to complete a task. Effort is measured in hours, days, or weeks depending on how much work is needed.

- Effort is calculated as follows:

Effort = Number of people x Hours per person

For instance, consider the bookshelf assembly task. Suppose it takes you 2 hours to assemble half of the bookshelf and your friend another 2 hours to complete the remaining work. You can now use the formula to calculate the effort in hours as follows:

Effort = 2 people x 2 hours

Effort = 4 hours

- Duration refers to the total time it takes to complete a task, usually measured in calendar time. Duration is not a measure of the task’s difficulty, or the number of people involved, but simply the calendar time needed for completion.
- Since duration depends on how the work is distributed, it is calculated using the following formula:

Duration = Effort ÷ (Number of people x Hours per person per day)

Here, hours per person per day refer to the total time a person can spend on the task in one day.

For instance, think about the bookshelf assembly task. If the effort for the task is 4 hours and each person can spend 2 hours on the task in one day, then you can use the formula to calculate the duration as follows:

Duration = 4 hours ÷ (2 people x 2 hours per person per day)

Duration = 4 hours ÷ 4 hours per day

Duration = 1 day
- In this example with you and your friend each having 2 hours of effort, it’s possible that you can work on the task for 1 hour on Monday and 1 hour on Tuesday, and your friend can work on the task for 1 hour on Tuesday and 1 hour on Wednesday. In this case, the duration is 3 days because you also need to consider the scheduling and availability of each person. Even though the total effort remains the same, the duration extends when work is distributed. This highlights how resource allocation and work distribution impact the actual duration for task completion.
- How to estimate the effort and duration?
- Team size

Adding more people can help complete tasks quicker. However, remember that while more people might reduce a task’s duration, the overall effort (work required to complete the task) remains the same.
- Task complexity

In project management, the complexity of a task factors into the effort and duration needed to complete it. PMs must consider if a project requires varied skills, specialized knowledge, and significant workload capacity. They can plan the effort and duration accordingly.
- Dependencies

In a project, tasks are often interdependent. Certain activities can’t start until others are complete. So, when estimating time, PMs must account for such dependencies.

- Risk management is the systematic process of identifying, analyzing, prioritizing, and responding to potential risks that might impact a project. By proactively managing risks, PMs can minimize the impact of the risks and avoid delays.

- Common risks in a project

Risks are inherent in projects. While PMs can’t eliminate project risks entirely, they can anticipate, reduce, and mitigate many risks. Let’s review some common project risks.
- Lack of clarity

A risky risk! If the project stakeholders don’t clearly define the project goals, objectives, or requirements, the lack of clarity leads to confusion and misdirection.
- Scope change

A classic risk! When a client asks for changes, whether small or large, this can lead to rework and impact timeline and budget.
- Budget overruns

A serious risk! Projects run the risk of exceeding their allocated budget because of poor cost estimation, unexpected expenses, or scope creeps.
- Time delays

A major risk! Resourcing issues, technical challenges, or client delays can cause planned tasks to take more time than anticipated. This might impact the overall project timeline.
- Resource shortage

A typical risk! This includes both human and material resources. Key team members might leave, or critical materials might not be available when needed.
- Communication issues

A clear risk! Poor communication and lack of clarity can lead to errors, delays, and conflicts.
- Quality risks

An unacceptable risk! Factors such as untrained resources, deadline pressure, and faulty processes might lead to poor deliverable quality, leading to rework, increased costs, and dissatisfied clients.
- Why is risk management important?

PMs must understand that even a carefully planned project can encounter unexpected challenges, leading to major disruptions. This is why PMs need to proactively manage risks so that they can:

•
Minimize interruptions in the project

•
Control costs

•
Keep the project on schedule

•
Increase project success rate

•
Be ready to manage crisis
- The risk management process is a structured step-by-step approach that helps PMs systematically identify, evaluate, and respond to potential risks throughout a project’s lifecycle. The risk management process provides a clear framework for PMs to take proactive measures to minimize disruptions and address unexpected challenges.

- The outcome of the risk management process is a risk management plan, which serves as a roadmap for assessing and mitigating risks throughout the project.

The risk management plan is a detailed document that defines how the project team identifies, analyzes, and responds to risks; outlines the roles and responsibilities of team members managing risks; and establishes processes for risk management throughout the project.
- In summary, PMs follow five steps to create the risk management plan and effectively manage risks throughout the project.

•
Step 1: Identify risks – PMs uncover and document potential risks that can affect the project.

•
Step 2: Assess and prioritize risks – PMs prioritize the risks based on their potential impact.

•
Step 3: Analyze risks quantitatively – PMs conduct a quantitative analysis to measure the impact of prioritized risks on cost, time, and scope.

•
Step 4: Plan risk responses – PMs select appropriate responses and management strategies, such as avoidance, mitigation, transfer, or acceptance.

•
Step 5: Monitor and adapt the approach – PMs continuously track status and update the approaches to manage the identified risks throughout the project lifecycle.

What is scope creep?
Let’s review what scope creep means.

Scope creep refers to the gradual expansion of project scope beyond its original objectives, requirements, or deliverables, without corresponding adjustments to time, budget, or resources. Scope creep typically occurs when new features, tasks, or requirements are added to a project after it is initialized and planned, often without proper evaluation of their impact on the project timeline and resources.

Scope creep directly affects task management because the project team needs to complete unplanned work with the limited resources, making it difficult to complete the planned tasks. In simpler words, when new tasks are added without extra time, people, or money to complete them, the project team struggles to complete the original planned work efficiently.

- Key concepts

1
During the monitor and control the project phase, project managers ensure that the team executes the project according to the project objectives.

2
Project monitoring is the process of continuously tracking, recording, and evaluating the progress of project activities so that PMs can keep the clients and internal stakeholders informed of the project’s status.

3
Project controlling is the process of taking necessary actions to address issues or changes identified through monitoring. PMs repeat the project controlling process many times throughout the project lifecycle by following four steps:

Step 1: Establish standards

Step 2: Observe performance

Step 3: Compare planned with actual performance

Step 4: Take corrective action

4
PMs manage the project’s team schedule according to the baseline plan and if required, adjust the baseline plan. To manage the team’s schedule, PMs gather progress updates, track and compare progress, identify and mitigate risks, and report progress.

5
Scope creep refers to the gradual expansion of project scope beyond its original objectives, requirements, or deliverables, without corresponding adjustments to time, budget, or resources. To manage the project team’s tasks and avoid scope creep, PMs update the project’s status and monitor its progress, identify deviations from plan, and implement change control.

6
The project makes more profit if PMs work within the budget. To manage the project costs, PMs follow several strategies, such as creating a budget plan, monitoring project expenses and costs, and optimizing resource allocation.

7
Change management is a formal process that helps PMs record, track, and resolve changes that occur during the project execution phase, ensuring they are accepted, deferred, or rejected. PMs effectively manage changes by following four steps:

Step 1: Identify change

Step 2: Investigate the change

Step 3: Estimate costs

Step 4: Approve the change

8
A project status report is a formal document that provides updates on the progress of a project, compares it to the project plan, lists planned actions, and highlights issues or risks. PMs create a project status report that includes various sections to keep the stakeholders and team members informed.

- Phase 4: Close the project

Imagine you’ve just completed delivering a new app to a client. The client is satisfied, and the users are downloading and actively using the app. Your job is done! But, is it really?

Before you move on to the next project, there are a few important steps you should take. You should review what went well and what can be done better in the next project. This is the close the project phase in the project management lifecycle.

The close the project phase isn’t just about finishing the work; it’s about accounting for every detail, from measuring outcomes against objectives and managing scope creep to documenting success and releasing resources for future projects. This phase provides closure to the project and leaves a lasting, positive impact, much like the joy of getting positive user reviews on the app.
- Step 1: Complete documentation and archive files
Documenting and archiving files at the end of a project is important for several key reasons.
Historical record

The project information goes into the organizational archives. PMs can refer to this project’s history in the future for various reasons, such as referencing, audits, updates, or legal requirements.
Lessons learned

Proper documentation allows the project team to capture lessons learned. This helps the team identify what worked well and what didn’t, providing valuable insights for improving future projects.
Knowledge transfer

PMs can use the archived files as a reference or training material for new team members or other departments in the organization.
Compliance

Many industries have regulatory requirements for documentation. Proper archiving ensures compliance with these regulations and standards.
Project continuity

In case PMs need to revisit the project or extend it in the future, having all documents and files archived ensures that new team members can efficiently familiarize themselves with the project.

Client satisfaction

Comprehensive documentation can help address future questions or disputes from clients or stakeholders.
- Ways of archiving projects

Depending on the nature of the project, PMs might select to archive files digitally in cloud-based storage or physically in cabinets. While archiving, PMs apply the following guidelines:

•
Save and store digital files to external hard drives or backup servers.

•
Use an organized filing system with labels and cabinets for printed materials.

•
Use project management software tools for convenience and efficiency.

•
Use standard templates and checklists for consistency and completeness.

•
Create a detailed project closure report with key information, lessons learned, and outcomes.

•
Archive important email communications related to the project in a format that allows easy search and retrieval.

- Step 2: Conduct a lessons learned session
Ryan is in a lessons learned meeting with his project team for GreenBambs PR campaign. The team has completed the project quite successfully.

The campaign was a mix of challenges and wins, from last-minute design changes to an impressive launch of the product through top media channels. Now, it’s time to share experiences.

Primary focus areas

The lessons learned session isn’t just about closing the project; it becomes a strong repository of actionable insights for the next big project. These insights become clear when PMs focus on a few primary areas.

Select each section to explore the primary focus areas for a lessons learned meeting.


Project objectives and scope
The project team evaluates the overall project to:

Review if the project objectives and scope were clearly defined and met.

Understand the scope changes and their impact on the project.

Compare the outcomes with the objectives.

Example: GreenBambs revised the core message of the campaign during the design phase. This led to Ryan’s team updating the project objectives, strategy, and deliverables. As a result, the initiate and plan phase of the project required more effort than Ryan had originally planned.


Project planning and execution
The team assesses the project’s plan and execution to:

Analyze the effectiveness of the project planning process.

Evaluate how well the project plan was executed and adhered to.

Discuss deviations from the plan and how they were managed.

Example: Ryan’s project planning was robust. After he experienced scope change in the design phase, he added buffer time during project execution. Project leads appreciated this.


Stakeholder engagement and communication
The team reviews stakeholder involvement to:

Assess the level of engagement and communication with clients and stakeholders throughout the project.

Identify what worked well in managing stakeholder expectations and what could be improved.

Example: According to Ryan’s communication management plan, he was the single point of contact for all communication with GreenBambs. However, as the project progressed, a few leads also started sending updates and deliverables, which caused some confusion among the client’s team. The team noted this point for future projects and decided to reevaluate having multiple points of contact.


Team performance and collaboration
The team assesses their teamwork to:

Evaluate the performance of the project team and their collaboration.

Identify skill gaps and training needs.

Discuss team dynamics, motivation, and other issues that arose.

Example: Ryan applauded the team’s effort in delivering the GreenBambs PR campaign. He also noted that some vendors needed training on their processes.


Risk management
The team analyzes how they addressed the project risks to:

Review the risk management process, including risk identification, assessment, and mitigation.

Identify how they handled the unforeseen risks.

Example: Ryan’s quality lead voiced her concern about incomplete legal documentation provided by GreenBambs that led to a delay in quality approval. The team discussed how to best handle similar challenges in future projects.


Resource management
The team evaluates the project’s resources to:

Assess the allocation and usage of resources, including time, budget, and people.

Identify how they managed the resource constraints.

Example: Managing resources was a major challenge in Ryan’s project. The team had to accommodate a delay in the design phase, which required adding more team members during execution. Ryan and his team had to pivot by working with a vendor partner to scale up the team.


Quality management
The team reviews the project’s overall quality performance to:

Review the quality assurance and quality control processes.

Assess whether the project deliverables met the required quality standards.

Example: The vendor team members assigned to the quality assurance team were not familiar with the process. Their initial work did not meet quality standards, so the QA lead had to spend some extra effort to upskill them.


Client and stakeholder satisfaction
The team evaluates the overall project feedback to:

Assess the client and stakeholders’ satisfaction level with the project outcomes.

Identify areas where expectations were not met.

Example: Ryan happily shared that GreenBambs had given five stars to the team. The client was happy with the project’s execution but provided feedback on key expectations for future projects, such as maintaining a single point of contact with their PM.


Process improvement
The team discusses the overall process to:

Identify improvements in processes or methodologies for future projects.

Capture suggestions for best practices and process enhancements.

Example: The Graphic team suggested agile reviews by internal stakeholders of deliverables to save time. The Marketing team proposed adding a “mock” strategy presentation step for new clients.

A lessons learned meeting is truly a place where everyone learns!
- Step 3: Celebrate success and release the team
A team that works hard and celebrates the successful completion of a project is a happy team!

But closing a project isn’t just about celebrating; it’s also about ensuring a smooth transition for the team.

So why is this final step important? Let’s find out.

Celebrating success is important because:

•
It boosts morale and promotes a positive work environment.

•
It reinforces a sense of accomplishment.

•
It creates a culture of appreciation and motivation.

A well-organized release of the team is helpful because:

•
It provides a proper closure to the completed project.

•
It enables the team to transition smoothly to the next project.

•
It enhances individual job satisfaction and strengthens loyalty.

Best practices to close the project

The closing the project phase is an opportunity for PMs to formally conclude the project.

Review the following list of best practices that PMs focus on when closing the project.


Celebrate success

Organize a celebration event or meeting.

Recognize the team’s hard work and accomplishments.


Release project resources

Officially release team members from the project.

Reallocate equipment and materials to other projects or return them to inventory.


Communicate project closure

Inform team members about the project closure.

Share key achievements with stakeholders.

Provide a final project report summarizing outcomes, metrics, recommendations, and lessons learned.


Complete administrative closure

Confirm the archival of all project documents.

Close out all financial accounts.

Update internal systems with project closure details and lessons learned.


Ensure transition and support

Perform proper knowledge transfer to maintenance teams of the project.

Provide necessary training or support documentation.

Back up project-related data and store it securely for future reference.

By systematically addressing each of these areas, PMs can ensure a thorough and successful project closure, setting the stage for future success.
