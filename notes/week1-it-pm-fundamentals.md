## Project Management Fundamentals (Reference Guide)

## Executive Summary
Project management delivers **business value** by coordinating people, scope, schedule, and budget through a disciplined lifecycle. Effective PMs choose an appropriate **delivery framework** (Waterfall, Agile, or Hybrid), then use planning artifacts and routines (WBS, baseline plan, status reporting) to keep work predictable and transparent. Across all approaches, strong outcomes depend on **stakeholder alignment**, **early risk management**, **clear communication**, and **controlled change**.

---

## Key Methodologies

### Project vs. Program vs. Portfolio
- **Project:** A **temporary** and **unique** effort with constraints that delivers value.
- **Program:** A collection of related projects managed together to achieve broader benefits.
- **Portfolio:** A group of projects, programs, and ongoing operations managed together to meet strategic goals (items may not be directly related, but are strategically aligned).

### Frameworks mentioned (how work is delivered)
A **project management framework** is a standardized set of guidelines, processes, and tools that helps teams work consistently across the lifecycle.

#### Comparison table

| Framework | Best when… | How work flows | Strengths | Watch-outs |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Waterfall** | Requirements are stable and approvals are sequential | Linear phases completed in order | Predictable; easier forecasting; strong documentation | Slow to adapt; issues often found later |
| **Agile** | Requirements evolve; feedback is frequent; value can be delivered in increments | Iterative cycles with frequent review and refinement | Adaptable; continuous feedback; earlier validation | Requires engaged stakeholders; can drift without discipline |
| **Hybrid** | Some work is fixed and some benefits from iteration | Mix of sequential planning + iterative delivery | Balances structure and flexibility | Can become unclear if “fixed vs iterative” isn’t explicit |

### Waterfall (phases referenced)
- **Analyze requirements**
- **Design**
- **Develop**
- **Test**
- **Deploy**

### Agile (phases referenced)
- **Conceptualize and initiate**
- **Plan for iterative development**
- **Develop and execute**
- **Review and gather feedback**
- **Release and improve continuously**

### Hybrid (phases referenced)
- **Analyze requirements**
- **Design**
- **Develop and release iteratively**

### How to choose a framework (practical criteria)
Consider:
- **Project complexity and size**
- **Stakeholder involvement level**
- **Scope clarity** (fixed vs likely to change)
- **Time and resource constraints**

## The Project Lifecycle

> Standard lifecycle phases: **Initiation, Planning, Execution, Monitoring & Controlling, Closing**.

### Initiation
**Purpose:** Define what the project is, why it matters, and who is involved.

#### Key documents
- **Project Charter:** A formal document that authorizes the project and clarifies purpose, objectives, stakeholders, risks, constraints, milestones, budget (high level), and ownership.
- **Project Canvas:** A visual overview of core elements (goals, users, benefits, team, stakeholders, scope, deliverables, activities, milestones, risks).

#### Initiation checklist
- [ ] Define the **purpose** and **high-level objectives**
- [ ] Identify key **stakeholders** (internal and external)
- [ ] Confirm **sponsor** and decision makers
- [ ] Draft and approve the **Project Charter**
- [ ] Create a **Project Canvas** for quick alignment

### Planning
**Purpose:** Translate objectives into a workable plan and set the baseline.

#### The project management triangle (triple constraint)
The **project management triangle** highlights the relationship between:
- **Scope** (work required / deliverables)
- **Time** (schedule)
- **Cost** (budget)

Changes in one typically force changes in one or both of the others.

#### Planning deliverables and activities
- **Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)**
- Effort and duration estimates
- Schedule and dependencies (often visualized via a **Gantt chart**)
- Cost estimates (resources, materials, contingency)
- Baseline plan (approved scope/schedule/budget)
- Communication management plan
- Risk management plan
- Team structure and resourcing (including outsourcing where appropriate)

#### Effort vs. Duration (do not mix these up)
- **Effort** = total work required
  - **Effort = Number of people × Hours per person**
- **Duration** = total elapsed time on the calendar
  - **Duration = Effort ÷ (Number of people × Hours per person per day)**

#### Baseline plan (how to finalize and use it)
- [ ] Confirm timelines and resources are realistic
- [ ] Review the budget vs scope for alignment
- [ ] Confirm deliverables and deadlines
- [ ] Compare actuals vs baseline once execution starts
- [ ] Update baseline only via change control

#### Communication management plan (what it must define)
- **Type of communication**
- **Frequency and key dates**
- **Sender/owner**
- **Receivers/audience**
- **Expectations** (so “who needs to know what” is explicit)

#### Risk management (common risks referenced)
- Lack of clarity
- Scope change
- Budget overruns
- Time delays
- Resource shortage
- Communication issues
- Quality risks

#### Risk management process (5 steps referenced)
- [ ] Identify risks
- [ ] Assess and prioritize
- [ ] Analyze risks quantitatively
- [ ] Plan responses (avoid / mitigate / transfer / accept)
- [ ] Monitor and adapt throughout the project

### Execution
**Purpose:** Deliver the work, manage the team, and maintain quality.

#### Execution activities (examples referenced)
- Development (building the deliverable)
- Testing and quality assurance
- Documentation
- Resource management
- Implementation / deployment

#### PM responsibilities during execution (high-level)
- [ ] Track progress against timelines and adjust schedules when needed
- [ ] Ensure deliverables are completed on time and within budget
- [ ] Coordinate across teams and resolve blockers
- [ ] Maintain regular stakeholder communication
- [ ] Manage risks and issues per the risk plan
- [ ] Oversee quality assurance and corrective actions
- [ ] Evaluate change requests and assess impact on scope/time/cost

#### Project kickoff meeting (what to cover)
- [ ] Introductions
- [ ] Background and objectives
- [ ] Scope and deliverables (what is **in-scope** vs **out-of-scope**)
- [ ] Roles and responsibilities
- [ ] High-level schedule and milestones
- [ ] Ways of working (communication, tools, cadence)

#### High-performing teams (characteristics referenced)
- Shared vision and commitment
- Open communication and collaboration
- Mutual trust and respect
- Clear roles and responsibilities
- Supportive environment

#### Leading and developing the team (guidelines referenced)
- [ ] Ensure alignment with project goals
- [ ] Provide support and feedback
- [ ] Encourage collaboration
- [ ] Manage risks and issues early
- [ ] Celebrate achievements

#### Remote teams (benefits and constraints referenced)
- **Benefits:** varied perspectives, cost efficiency, potentially faster cycle across time zones
- **Constraints:** communication barriers, coordination difficulty, team unity challenges

#### Cross-cultural teams (guidelines referenced)
- [ ] Use clear, direct language (avoid idioms and unnecessary jargon)
- [ ] Build cultural awareness and respect working norms
- [ ] Encourage inclusive participation and decision making
- [ ] Stay flexible with time zones and work habits
- [ ] Create team bonding opportunities

#### Meetings (types referenced)
- Kickoff meeting
- Status update meetings
- Stakeholder engagement meetings
- Closing and review meetings
- Event-related / emergency meetings

#### Meeting discipline (guidelines referenced)
- [ ] Create a detailed agenda (topics, time, speakers)
- [ ] Limit attendees to those who impact outcomes
- [ ] Start and end on time; timebox discussions
- [ ] Ensure full participation
- [ ] Maintain structure (notes, decisions, follow-ups)
- [ ] Document and share proceedings

#### Quality management (QA vs QC)
- **Quality Assurance (QA):** proactive process to prevent failures by following the quality plan.
- **Quality Control (QC):** reactive process to identify failures through audits/testing after work is produced.

Quality management steps (referenced):
- [ ] Establish a quality plan
- [ ] Define quality standards
- [ ] Set success metrics
- [ ] Define QA and QC processes
- [ ] Assign roles
- [ ] Monitor quality-related tasks

### Monitoring & Controlling
**Purpose:** Keep the project aligned to the baseline plan and respond to deviations.

#### Monitoring vs controlling
- **Monitoring:** tracking, recording, and evaluating progress (collect data + compare to plan).
- **Controlling:** taking corrective actions based on what monitoring shows.

#### Controlling loop (repeatable)
- [ ] Establish standards (baseline)
- [ ] Observe performance (reports, meetings, audits)
- [ ] Compare planned vs actual
- [ ] Take corrective action and communicate impacts

#### Managing schedule during monitoring
- [ ] Gather progress updates
- [ ] Track and compare progress vs baseline
- [ ] Identify and mitigate schedule risks
- [ ] Report progress and escalate when needed

#### Scope creep
**Scope creep** is uncontrolled expansion of scope without corresponding changes in time, budget, or resources.

Controls to reduce scope creep:
- [ ] Keep task status updated in a tracker
- [ ] Hold regular status meetings to surface delays early
- [ ] Identify deviations from the baseline plan quickly
- [ ] Implement and enforce change control

#### Cost management (core strategies referenced)
- [ ] Create a budget plan (labor, materials, contingency)
- [ ] Monitor expenses regularly (ideally in real time)
- [ ] Compare actuals vs budget and analyze variances
- [ ] Control changes that impact cost
- [ ] Optimize resource allocation and avoid unnecessary spend

#### Change management (4 steps referenced)
- [ ] Identify change (document request and clarify scope)
- [ ] Investigate impact (scope, schedule, budget, alternatives)
- [ ] Estimate costs (financial and resource implications)
- [ ] Approve change (update plans and communicate)

#### Status reporting
A **project status report** provides updates, compares progress to plan, and highlights actions, risks, and issues.

Typical sections referenced:
- Project information
- Status summary
- Progress report (completed + upcoming work)
- Milestones
- Project health (budget)
- Change management log
- Conclusions and recommendations

Best practices referenced:
- Keep it clear and brief
- Use visuals when helpful
- Tailor detail to the audience
- Be consistent in cadence
- Focus on action items and decisions

### Closing
**Purpose:** Complete administrative closure, capture learning, and release resources.

#### Closeout steps (3 steps referenced)
- [ ] Complete documentation and archive files
- [ ] Conduct a lessons learned session
- [ ] Celebrate success and release the team/resources

#### Why archiving matters (reasons referenced)
- Historical record and auditability
- Lessons learned capture
- Knowledge transfer
- Compliance support
- Project continuity (future changes/extensions)
- Client satisfaction and dispute support

#### Lessons learned session (focus areas referenced)
- Project objectives and scope
- Planning and execution
- Stakeholder engagement and communication
- Team performance and collaboration
- Risk management
- Resource management
- Quality management
- Client/stakeholder satisfaction
- Process improvements

## Glossary of Terms
- **Baseline plan:** Approved scope/schedule/budget used to measure actual performance.
- **Change management:** Formal process to record, assess, approve/reject, and implement changes.
- **Communication management plan:** Defines communication types, cadence, senders, receivers, and expectations.
- **Contingency fund:** Budget reserved for unexpected costs.
- **Cross-cultural team:** Team with members from different cultural backgrounds.
- **Dependencies:** Task relationships that define sequencing constraints.
- **Duration:** Calendar time to complete work.
- **Effort:** Total work required to complete a task.
- **Gantt chart:** Visual timeline of tasks, dates, and dependencies.
- **Hybrid framework:** Blends Waterfall and Agile elements.
- **Kickoff meeting:** Formal start meeting to align team and stakeholders.
- **Monitoring:** Tracking and evaluating actual progress against plan.
- **Controlling:** Corrective action based on monitoring results.
- **Portfolio:** Strategic grouping of projects/programs/operations.
- **Program:** Group of related projects managed for broader benefit.
- **Project charter:** Document that formally authorizes a project and sets direction.
- **Project canvas:** Visual snapshot of key project elements.
- **Project management triangle:** Scope–Time–Cost relationship.
- **Quality assurance (QA):** Preventive activities to reduce defects.
- **Quality control (QC):** Detection activities to identify defects in outputs.
- **Remote team:** Distributed team collaborating via digital tools.
- **Risk management plan:** Defines how risks are identified, assessed, responded to, and monitored.
- **Scope creep:** Uncontrolled scope expansion without adjusting constraints.
- **Stakeholders:** Individuals/groups affected by or influencing the project.
- **WBS (Work Breakdown Structure):** Hierarchical breakdown of project scope.
- **Waterfall:** Sequential framework with phases completed in order.

## Actionable Takeaways (Cheat Sheet)

### The “must-remember” formulas
- **Effort = Number of people × Hours per person**
- **Duration = Effort ÷ (Number of people × Hours per person per day)**

### Triple constraint: practical rules
- If you change **scope**, expect impact to **time and/or cost**.
- If the **deadline is fixed**, protect the plan by tightening scope and controlling change.
- If the **budget is fixed**, trade scope and delivery approach before you trade quality.

### Risk responses (fast recall)
- **Avoid**: change the plan to remove the risk
- **Mitigate**: reduce likelihood or impact
- **Transfer**: shift responsibility/impact to a third party
- **Accept**: acknowledge and plan contingencies

### Scope creep prevention checklist
- [ ] Write scope in plain language (**in-scope / out-of-scope**)
- [ ] Define **“done”** with measurable success criteria
- [ ] Use a change request process for any scope additions
- [ ] Keep a visible list of top risks and review it regularly

### Reusable PM checklist
- [ ] Who is the sponsor and who approves scope changes?
- [ ] What is the enforcement/cutover date (if any)?
- [ ] What are the top 3 risks and how will I reduce them?
- [ ] How will I report status weekly (format + audience)?
